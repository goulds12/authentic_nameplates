About

The tradition of naming houses dates back many centuries in Britain and throughout Europe. In Australia, it is as old as white settlement, with the early settlers bringing with them a piece of their background in the form of a house name. It often revealed something of the person's country or town of origin, their own language and sometimes their aspirations.

House names were common during a time when there was no street numbering system in place. It was a means of identifying a house for the benefit of visitors, home delivery services and the postman. A home took on an identity and character and it became known by its name.

Book

Nameplate maker Graham Gould explains how the tradition began and traces trends over the decades of European settlement.
If your house was built before 1930, then it possibly had a name. Graham Gould leads you through the process of researching the original name of your home, giving practical advice on how to locate the historical sources.
And if your house doesn't have a name, Graham Gould provides them - he lists thousands of names of all kinds, from historical to humorous, Aboriginal to Swahili. He gives advice on choosing a name for your house, and supplies dozens of ideas on how you can create a personalised name.
